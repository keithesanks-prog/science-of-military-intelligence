### Practical Explainer: The Topography of Orbit
(Visual: A cross-section of Earth with concentric rings and a single elongated, egg-shaped loop. Labels appear as the camera pans out.)

Narrator: "To understand how we see the world, we have to understand the 'map' of the sky. Space isn't just one place—it's a topography of speeds and distances."

(Visual: The innermost ring, very close to Earth.)
Narrator: "**Low Earth Orbit (LEO)**. 160 to 2,000 kilometers up. This is the 'Sprinting Zone.' Satellites here move at 17,000 miles per hour, circling the Earth in just 90 minutes. It's perfect for high-resolution cameras, but they pass by so fast they only see a sliver of the world at a time."

(Visual: A middle ring, further out.)
Narrator: "**Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)**. 2,000 to 35,000 kilometers. This is the 'Steady Zone,' home to the GPS constellation. It's the middle ground—high enough to see a wide area, low enough to keep a strong signal."

(Visual: A distant, perfectly circular ring.)
Narrator: "**Geostationary Orbit (GEO)**. Exactly 35,786 kilometers above the equator. In this 'Stationary Sentinel' zone, the satellite's speed matches the Earth's rotation. To someone on the ground, the satellite appears to hang perfectly still in the sky, watching the same spot forever."

(Visual: An elongated, egg-shaped orbit that reaches high over the North Pole.)
Narrator: "**Highly Elliptical Orbit (HEO)**. Also known as the **Molniya** orbit. It’s an 'Egg-shaped' path. The satellite 'lingers' high over the northern hemisphere for hours, peering down at the Arctic and Russia where GEO satellites can't reach, before diving back down for a quick whip around the Earth."

### The Digital Fingerprint (The Anatomy of a Shout)
(Visual: A rhythmic, buzzing sound like an old dial-up modem. On-screen, we see a green oscilloscope line jumping. Labels appear: **PRF (Pulse Repetition Frequency)**, **Pulse Width**, and **Scan Rate**.)

Narrator: "What did GRAB actually see? It didn't see a plane or a building. It saw the **Digital Fingerprint** of a radar. Every radar system in the world has a 'shout' that is unique to its design."

(Visual: A graphic appears with the equation: **PRF = 1 / PRI**. Underneath, the Soviet 'Token' radar is analyzed. **PRF: 375 pps**. **Pulse Width (τ): 2 microseconds**.)

Narrator: "To identify a signal, we use the math of the pulse. First is the **Pulse Repetition Frequency (PRF)**—the beat of the radar's heart. It is simply one divided by the interval between pulses. Then there is the **Duty Cycle**."

(Visual: A new equation appears: **Duty Cycle (D) = τ / PRI**. Two bars appear: one showing the short 'shout' ($τ$) and one showing the long 'silence' ($PRI$).)

Narrator: "The Duty Cycle tells us how much energy is being pushed into the sky. It’s the ratio of the shout ($τ$) to the silence ($PRI$). By combining these numbers, analysts can identify exactly what kind of system was looking at them. From space, these equations turn a buzz into a weapon's name."

### Act 3: The Intersection of Time

### Practical Explainer: The Speed of Light Delay (Latency)
(Visual: A signal pulse leaving a ground transmitter and heading to three different orbits: LEO, MEO, and GEO. Clocks appear over each.)

Narrator: "This topography of distance creates a fundamental problem: **Latency**. Even at 186,000 miles per second ($c$), space is so vast that signals take time to travel. The math is a simple ratio: **Time ($t$) = Distance ($d$) / $c$**."

(Visual: 
**LEO (500 km):** 1.67 milliseconds.
**MEO (20,000 km):** 67 milliseconds.
**GEO (35,786 km):** 119 milliseconds.)

Narrator: "A signal to a LEO spy satellite is nearly instantaneous. But to reach a GEO sentinel, the signal takes over a tenth of a second for a one-way trip. By the time you send a command and get a 'Roger' back, a quarter of a second has vanished. In the world of high-speed signals, that delay is a lifetime."

Narrator: "But this delay isn't just a hurdle; it's a ruler. Because if you know exactly when a signal was sent, and exactly when it arrived at these different 'altitudes,' you can solve for the sender's exact location. This is the foundation of the **Intersection of Time**."

### Mathematical Ghost Deep Dive: The TDOA Hyperbola
(Visual: An animation of three satellites in a triangle over a single radio emitter on the ground. A signal 'pulse' leaves the emitter and reaches each satellite at a slightly different microsecond.)

Narrator: "How do you find a needle in a haystack from 10,000 miles away? You use the math of the **Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA)**. When an enemy radar pulses, its signal reaches Satellite A first, then Satellite B a few microseconds later."

(Visual: A graphic appears showing the hyperbolic equation: **||r - s₁|| - ||r - s₂|| = c · Δt**. A curved 'Lane of Possibility' is drawn on the map.)

Narrator: "The difference in that timing ($\Delta t$) creates a 'Lane of Possibility'—a mathematical curve called a hyperbola. This equation tells us the exact set of points where the distance difference between two satellites is constant."

(Visual: Three blue hyperbolas are drawn. They intersect at a single, glowing red point.)

Narrator: "By comparing the timing between three satellites, those hyperbolas intersect at a single point. This is the 'Intersection of Time.' You don't need a camera to see the enemy; you just need to solve for the intersection."

### Practical Explainer: The Velocity of the High Ground (The Math of Impact)
(Visual: A satellite orbiting Earth. A vector arrow appears, labeled **Velocity ($v$)**. The equation appears: **v = √(GM / r)**. Beside it, a comparison: **Bullet: 800 m/s** vs **Orbital Debris: 7,800 m/s**.)

Narrator: "To understand why a tiny shard of metal is so deadly, we have to look at the math of the 'Magic Number.' To stay in Low Earth Orbit and not fall back to Earth, an object must maintain a velocity of approximately **17,500 miles per hour**—that's **7.8 kilometers per second**."

Narrator: "The equation for this is **$v$ equals the square root of $G$ (the gravitational constant) times $M$ (the mass of Earth), divided by $r$ (the distance from the center of the Earth)**. In the vacuum of space, there is no air to slow you down. Once you reach that speed, you keep it forever."

(Visual: A marble-sized piece of metal. Beside it, a 16lb bowling ball. The marble zooms past at 17,500 mph, shattering a satellite panel. The equation appears: **Eₖ = ½mv²**)

Narrator: "Because velocity is squared in the kinetic energy equation (**$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$**), speed matters much more than mass. A tiny, marble-sized piece of debris moving at orbital velocity carries the same destructive energy as a **bowling ball moving at 60 miles per hour**. At these speeds, even a fleck of paint can punch a hole through a reinforced satellite hull."
