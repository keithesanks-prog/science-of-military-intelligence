### Mathematical Ghost: The Shift Equation
(Visual: The equation appears: **c = (m + k) mod 26**. We see the letter 'P' (15) shifted by a key 'K' (10) to become 'Z' (25).)

Narrator: "The math is simple: take your message ($m$), add a secret key ($k$), and use the **mod 26** operator to keep the result within the alphabet. It’s like a clock that only has 26 hours. If you go past Z, you just start back at A."

(Visual: The British 'Room 40' analyzing the code. The Vigenère square appears.)

Narrator: "But a simple shift is easy to break with frequency analysis—the counting of the ghosts. To stop the British, we used a **Polyalphabetic Wall**. We used the **Vigenère Cipher**."

### Mathematical Ghost: The Polyalphabetic Wall
(Visual: The equation: **ci = (pi + ki) mod 26**. An animation shows a repeated keyword like 'SECRET' shifting each letter of the message differently.)

Narrator: "Now, every letter ($p_i$) is shifted by its own unique key ($k_i$) from a repeating word. This flattened the 'ghosts' of frequency analysis, making a 'whisper' look like pure noise. For centuries, this was the 'Indecipherable Cipher.' Until the math of machines arrived."

Act 2: The Standardization War (S-Box Math)
(Visual: The 1970s. The transition from paper to silicon. The DES algorithm appears as a flow chart of XOR gates and 'S-Boxes'.)

Narrator: "In 1973, we needed a lock for a digital world. IBM proposed the Data Encryption Standard (DES). It used a **Substitution-Permutation Network**."

### Mathematical Ghost: The S-Box Mystery
(Visual: 8 blocks of 6 bits entering a transformation table. They come out as 4 bits each.)

Narrator: "The heart of the digital vault is the **S-Box**. It’s a non-linear look-up table. You put in one value, and you get out something completely different. It’s the mathematical equivalent of a blender. Even a tiny change in the input creates an explosion of change in the output—this is what we call **Diffusion**."

Act 3: The Public Key Revolution (1976)
(Visual: Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman. Animation of a box that is easy to lock but impossible to unlock without the name of a trapdoor.)

Narrator: "The problem with DES was the key. Both sides had to share it. In 1976, we found the **Asymmetric Paradox**: The math of the One-Way Function."

### Mathematical Ghost: The Prime Fortress (RSA)
(Visual: Two massive prime numbers, **p** and **q**, multiplying to form **N**. A clock appears representing **mod N**.)

Narrator: "This is **RSA**. It’s built on the **Prime Factorization Paradox**. It is computationally easy to multiply two massive primes to get a huge number ($N$). But it is nearly impossible for a computer to work backwards and find those primes."

(Visual: The encryption equation: **c = m^e mod N**. The decryption: **m = m^d mod N**.)

Narrator: "We share the **Public Key** ($e$, $N$) with the world. Anyone can lock a message using modular exponentiation. But only the person with the **Private Key** ($d$) can undo the math. This 'Trapdoor' is what allows you to buy a coffee or send an encrypted text without ever meeting the recipient."
