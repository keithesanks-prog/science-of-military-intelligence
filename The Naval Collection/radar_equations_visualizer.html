Principle 1 The Geometry of the Beam
W = 2 • R • tan(θ/2)

First, let’s look at the shape of our radar. People think of radar as a 'corridor' of light, but it’s actually a cone. As the range (R) increases, the beamwidth (θ) forces the beam to expand into a physical width (W). This isn't a glitch; it's a law of physics called Diffraction.

Breaking Down the Formula: $W = 2 \cdot R \cdot \tan(\theta/2)$

If you imagine the radar beam as a triangle, $R$ is the length from the tip to the base. We take the total angle $\theta$ and cut it in half: $\theta/2$. The "Opposite" side of that right triangle is half of our total width: $W/2$. The "Adjacent" side is our Range: $R$.

Using the identity $\tan(\text{angle}) = \text{Opposite} / \text{Adjacent}$: $\tan(\theta/2) = \frac{W/2}{R}$

Rearrange to solve for $W$: $W = 2 \cdot R \cdot \tan(\theta/2)$.

Write: $W = \text{The size of the net}$

Analogy: Think of a Flashlight. When you shine it at a wall 2 feet away, the circle of light is small and bright. When you shine it at a building 100 yards away, the circle is huge but dim.

Principle 2 The Return Trip (R⁴ Law)
Pr ∝ 1/R⁴

This is the 'Fourth-Power Law.' It's the most brutal part of radar physics. To see a target, the energy has to travel out to the target (1/R²), and then the tiny echo has to travel all the way back (1/R²).

Write: $1/R^4 = \text{The Round-Trip Tax}$

Analogy: Imagine Shouting into a Grand Canyon. You have to shout loud enough for the sound to reach the far wall, but the wall 'shouts' back with only a fraction of that energy. Every time you double the distance, the signal doesn't just get 2x weaker—it gets 16x weaker.

Principle 3 The Electronic "Jump"
Δφ = (2πd/λ) sin(θs)

The SPY-1 doesn't have a motor; it doesn't spin. Instead, we use Phase Shifting (Δφ). By changing the timing of the electricity at each of these blue dots, we make the waves 'line up' in a specific direction. This is how the beam 'jumps' across the sky instantly.

Write: $\Delta\phi = \text{The Steering Wheel}$

Analogy: Think of The Wave at a stadium. The people aren't running around the track; they are just standing up and sitting down at the right time. Wave travels, people don't.

Principle 4 The Doppler Shift
fd = 2v / λ

Finally, we don't just see where the target is; we see how fast it's moving. When the beam hits a moving object, the frequency shifts.

Write: Blue Shift = Closing / Red Shift = Receding

Analogy: Think of a Race Car passing you. As it approaches, the engine sounds high-pitched ($EEEE$). As it passes, it drops to a low pitch ($OOOO$). The radar 'hears' that pitch change to calculate speed instantly.

Principle 5 Clarity through Repetition
SNR ∝ (Pt • n) / R⁴

The ocean is noisy—waves, rain, and birds create 'clutter.' To find a real target, we use Dwell Time (n). We hit the same spot multiple times and average the results. This builds up our Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) until the computer is sure it’s a real threat.

Write: n = The 'Are you sure?' button

Analogy: Imagine hearing a friend in a crowded bar. If they say their name once, you might miss it. If they repeat it 10 times, your brain 'integrates' those sounds and filters out the background music. That 10th repeat is your n.
