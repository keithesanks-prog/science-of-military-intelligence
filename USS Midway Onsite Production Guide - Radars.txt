# USS Midway Onsite Production Guide: Radar Special

This guide contains the onsite filming scripts and technical explainers for the radar systems of the USS Midway.

---

## Segment 1: The AN/SPS-48 (The Waffle Iron)
**(Action: Point the camera up at the massive, spinning square antenna on the high mast.)**

**Script:**
"Check out the 'Waffle Iron.' This is the AN/SPS-48, the Navy’s premier 3D Air Search Radar. Most civilian radars—like the ones at an airport—give you a flat 'blip' on a map. They tell you the direction and distance, but they leave out the most critical piece of data: Altitude.

If you’re a tactical commander, knowing an aircraft is 50 miles away is useless if you don’t know if it’s at 500 feet or 50,000 feet. The '48' solves that by turning a 2D map into a 3D volume of airspace.

**(Action: Zoom in on the horizontal 'slats' or rows on the face of the antenna.)**

How does a flat, square antenna 'see' up? Usually, you’d have to tilt the whole dish, but that’s too slow for modern threats. Instead, the SPS-48 uses Frequency Scanning.

Look at these horizontal slats. Each one is a waveguide. By shifting the Frequency ($f$) of the radar pulse just a tiny bit, the physics of the wave changes. This is the ultimate proof of the Planck-Einstein relation: $E = hf$.

In this system, the frequency literally determines the direction of the energy. A higher frequency 'bends' the beam up; a lower frequency 'bends' it down. It’s steering a beam of pure energy at the speed of light without moving a single mechanical part of the antenna face.

**(Action: Motion toward the horizon, then sweep your hand up toward the sky.)**

This thing is a beast. It can track a sea-skimming missile just a few feet above the waves to clear out the 'clutter,' but it can also 'squint' its eyes up to nearly 70 degrees.

We’re talking about a detection ceiling of 100,000 feet. That is nearly three times higher than a 747 cruises—it’s essentially the edge of space. It maps a 'bubble' around the ship 250 miles wide and 20 miles high. If a 'High Diver' missile tries to drop straight down on us from the stratosphere, the SPS-48 sees it coming before it even starts its dive.

**(Action: Walk toward the ship's superstructure or missile launchers if visible.)**

Why do we need this much precision? Because you can’t shoot what you can’t locate in 3D space. The SPS-48 provides the high-fidelity 'fire control' data needed to put a Standard Missile ($SM-2$) into a tiny 'basket' miles away so its own seeker can take over and finish the job.

It was the backbone of the 'New Threat Upgrade' ships and remains a legend in naval electronic warfare. High-energy physics, $E = hf$, and total situational awareness—all packed into a spinning waffle. This is Science Meets Intelligence. Stay tuned."

## Segment 2: The Sky-Watcher vs. The Surface Search
**(Action: Gesture toward the large, orange/black lattice billboard antenna (SPS-49), then toward the smaller "half-pipe" antenna (SPS-67).)**

**Script:**
"On a carrier like the Midway, you don't just have one set of eyes. You have specialized hunters. 

Look up high—that large, rotating lattice 'billboard' is the **AN/SPS-49**. This is the 'Sky-Watcher.' It uses long-wavelength L-Band frequencies to punch through 250 miles of atmosphere. It’s not looking for detail; it’s looking for *anything* incoming. It’s the long-range early warning system.

Now look lower, on the foremast—the 'half-pipe' antenna. That’s the **AN/SPS-67**. This is the 'Surface Search' radar. While the SPS-49 is looking at the stars, the 67 is focused on the water. Its job is to detect sea-skimming missiles, small boats, or even just buoys in the channel. It uses the C-Band—shorter waves that give it high precision for navigating tight waters."

## Segment 3: The EMI Battle (Interference)
**(Action: Stand with the island superstructure in the background, showing multiple radars.)**

**Script:**
"When you have multiple high-powered transmitters blasting microwave energy from the same mast, you have a massive engineering nightmare: EMI, or Electromagnetic Interference. How do they keep these systems from 'deafening' each other?

It’s a three-pronged defense:
1. **Frequency Isolation**: They speak in different voices. The SPS-49 speaks in a 'bass' (1-2 GHz), while the SPS-67 speaks in a 'whistle' (4-8 GHz). The receivers are tuned to only hear their own band.
2. **Blanking & Triggering**: This is the 'I'm Talking Now' method. When one radar fires a massive blast, it sends a digital trigger to the others, telling them to 'close their ears' for a few microseconds. It happens thousands of times a second.
3. **Polarization**: They orient the waves differently. One might send vertical waves, while the other sends horizontal. It’s like wearing polarized sunglasses to block glare—the antennas simply ignore the 'wrong' waves."

## Segment 4: Beam Shaping (The Headlight Analogy)
**(Action: Use a flashlight or your hand to demonstrate a beam pattern.)**

**Script:**
"Think about your car headlights. They aren't just round bulbs; they are shaped to throw light on the road without blinding oncoming drivers. Naval radars do the same thing. 

Look at the curve of the SPS-67 or the slats of the 48. They use a technique called **Cosecant-Squared Beam Shaping**. Usually, a radar beam gets weaker the further up it goes. But by shaping the energy, engineers ensure that the beam is evenly spread. 

It prevents 'dead zones'—making sure that whether a target is 5 miles away or 50 miles away, it returns the same signal strength. They aren't just throwing energy into the air; they are sculpting it to make sure the 'Bodyguard' has no blind spots."

## Segment 5: The Rate of Spin (Trade-offs of RPM)
**(Action: Stand near a revolving antenna, timing the rotation with your watch.)**

**Script:**
"Ever wonder why some radars spin like a ceiling fan while others take their time? On the Midway, the **Rate of Spin** (RPM) is a calculated trade-off between two things: **Update Rate** and **Sensitivity**.

Take the **AN/SPS-67** surface search radar. It spins relatively fast—around 15 to 30 RPM. Why? Because when you're navigating a 60,000-ton carrier or looking for sea-skimming missiles, you need a high update rate. You need to know where that threat is *now*, every two to four seconds.

But then look at the massive **AN/SPS-49** air search radar. It spins much slower—usually 6 or 12 RPM. In the radar world, a slower spin means more 'dwell time' on the target. The antenna gets to hit the target with more pulses in a single pass, which allows the receiver to pull a faint signal out of the background noise from much further away.

It’s the difference between a quick glance and a long stare. The '49' takes a long, deep stare at the horizon to see 250 miles out, while the '67' takes quick, frequent glances to make sure we don't hit anything in the harbor. It’s the engineering of speed vs. distance—and on the Midway, the mast is doing both at once."

## Segment 6: Pencil Beams vs. Fan Beams (The Physics of Elevation)
**(Action: Use your hands to show a wide, flat 'fan' shape, then a narrow 'pencil' shape.)**

**Script:**
"To really understand how the AN/SPS-48 gets that 3D data, you have to look at the shape of the energy it transmits. 

A standard 2D radar, like the SPS-49, sends out a **Fan Beam**. Think of it like a giant vertical slice of pie rotating around the ship. It’s wide from top to bottom, which is great for finding *if* something is there, but because the energy is spread out, it can’t tell you the height. To the radar, an aircraft at 5,000 feet and 50,000 feet looks exactly the same.

The SPS-48 changes the game by using **Pencil Beams**. Instead of one big fan, it transmits a rapid-fire sequence of narrow, highly focused 'pencils' of energy. 

This is where the transmission logic gets cool. Because it's a frequency-scanning radar, it sends out a series of pulses, each on a slightly different frequency. The first pulse might be at 2.9 GHz and its 'bent' at a 2-degree angle. The next is 3.0 GHz and aimed at 5 degrees. Within a fraction of a second, the radar has 'stacked' a dozen individual beams on top of each other, painting the sky from the horizon to the stratosphere.

When the signal bounces back, the computer doesn't just see a blip; it knows exactly which frequency (and therefore which angle) produced that return. That is the 'SMI' secret: it's not just one radar; it's a dozen mini-radars stacked vertically, all working at the speed of light."

## SMI Key Concepts for the Midway Visit:
1. **3D Radar (AN/SPS-48)**: Provides Range, Bearing, and Altitude.
2. **Frequency Scanning**: Using $E=hf$ physics to steer a beam up or down by shifting frequency without moving the antenna mechanically.
3. **EMI (Electromagnetic Interference)**: The noise created when multiple high-power sensors operate in close proximity.
4. **Blanking**: The process of momentarily shutting down a receiver to protect it from a nearby transmitter's pulse.
5. **Cosecant-Squared Pattern**: A specific beam shape that provides uniform signal returns across a wide range of altitudes and distances.
