### Mathematical Ghost Deep Dive: The Width of a Blink
(Visual: An animation of two planes flying very close together. A long, red radar pulse heads toward them. The pulse is so long it covers both planes at once. As it returns, the two echoes merge into one big, messy blob on the screen.)

Narrator: "In Episode 3, we learned how to find distance. But how do we find *Accuracy*? How do we know if we’re looking at one big ship or ten small missiles? This is **Range Resolution**, and it depends on a single factor: the length of your shout."

(Visual: A graphic appears: **ΔR = (c × τ) / 2**. The long pulse is replaced by a series of tiny, sharp 'clicks'—very short pulses.)

Narrator: "A radar pulse isn't a point; it’s a physical object made of waves. If your pulse is 1 microsecond long, it is physically 300 meters wide. If two targets are closer than half that distance—150 meters—their echoes will overlap. To the 'Radio Eye,' they are one single ghost."

(Visual: The tiny clicks hit the two planes. They return as two distinct, sharp pips.)

Narrator: "To see the detail, the 'Digital Sentry' has to send a shorter blink. The shorter the pulse ($\tau$), the higher the resolution ($\Delta R$). This is the science of precision. It’s why modern radar doesn't just see 'something' in the air—it can tell the difference between a bird's wing and a missile's fin by shrinking the width of its blink."
